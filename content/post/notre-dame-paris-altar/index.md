> I have loved, O Lord, the beauty of thy house; and the place where thy glory dwelleth. - Psalm 26:8

> Fashion has done more mischief than revolutions. It has cut to the quick - it has attacked the very bone and framework of the art. It has mangled, dislocated, killed the edifice - in its form as well as in its meaning - in its logic as well as in its beauty. - Victor Hugo

> Viollet-le-Duc not only criticized the past decisions and changes that occurred in the Cathedral but he would soon be himself, critiqued for his restoration ideas and design choices that would occur less than a century later. - Devrim Topsakal, 2023[^1]

The restoration of any artistic work - be it book, building, or painting - prompts the question: restoring it to what? 

The Cathedral Notre Dame de Paris took nearly a century to build (1163-1260), and architectural styles had already shifted during those years. King Louis XIV commissioned a major renovation of the choir (1699-1714). In the turbulent wake of the French Revolution, Napoleon's coronation, and Victor Hugo's novel, Eugene Viollet-le-Duc conducted extensive exterior and interior renovations. Further modern renovations took place in the late 19th and early 20th century, but that story remains to be told. 

The high altar and the choir of Notre Dame have gone through several drastic changes, all of which drew strong and mixed reactions when they happened, and in ensuing decades. 

Below is a pictorial timeline of the high altar and choir of Notre Dame de Paris, with as much attribution as I can manage. I arranged the files chronologically by the date they represented, not necessarily the date they were created.
